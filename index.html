Our Mission:

Given the complex and difficult to navigate nature of the American immigration system, this project attempts to provide migrants, their family members and other relevant parties a resource that will allow them to understand the circumstances under which they would be asked, or required, to provide DNA. This resource contains a number of “wayfinders” – graphical illustrations of the pathways through which migrants interact with the United States Citizenship and Immigration Services. Each wayfinder is intended to not only assist the interested parties in navigating the complex legal system surrounding the collection of DNA from migrants, but also to direct individuals to resources that may assist them in ensuring that the data they provide is retained in a manner that is safe and secure and is not used in a way that harms them or their family members. Additional wayfinders will be added to assist government officials (such as coroners) who find unidentified human remains on the border in their attempts to return those remains to the family members of the missing individual. There will also be additional wayfinders to assist the family members of missing migrants in their search to find their loved ones, including potentially identifying unidentified human remains found on the border.


The collection of DNA is a highly regulated process and it is important for individuals to understand not only why their data is being collected, but how it is being used and stored. The highly personal nature of DNA data demands that the agencies collecting and storing such data must do so in a manner that ensures the safety and legitimacy of the system, and does not expose individuals to undue risk of breach.


The project is a collaboration between researchers at Duke University, Northwestern University, University of Tennessee and Texas State University, funded through an NIH grant. The overall goal of the project is to map and analyze the impacts of the current immigration regime and the collection of DNA from migrants. At this time, the project is in a “beta” stage, but the expectation is that the resources will be made available to the public by the end of 2020.
